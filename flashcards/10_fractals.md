# Dynamical Systems — Fractals

## 10.1 What a Fractal Is

Q: What are the DEFINING characteristics of a FRACTAL?
A: (1) [Self-similarity]: the set looks similar at every scale (exact, approximate, or statistical). (2) [Fine structure]: detail visible at all magnifications — unlike smooth curves, which become straight when zoomed in. (3) [Non-integer dimension]: fractal dimension exceeds topological dimension. (4) [Not smoothly describable]: no simple equation for every point; often defined RECURSIVELY or ITERATIVELY. Not a strict mathematical definition — Mandelbrot deliberately left it loose.

## 10.2 The Cantor Set

C: The [Cantor set] is constructed by iteratively REMOVING the middle third of each interval: start with $[0, 1]$; remove $(1/3, 2/3)$; remove the middle thirds of $[0, 1/3]$ and $[2/3, 1]$; repeat ad infinitum.

Q: What are the KEY PROPERTIES of the Cantor set?
A: (1) UNCOUNTABLE (same cardinality as $\mathbb{R}$) despite having MEASURE ZERO. (2) TOTALLY DISCONNECTED — every pair of points separated by a gap. (3) NOWHERE DENSE — contains no interval. (4) PERFECT — every point is a limit point. (5) Fractal dimension $\log_3 2 \approx 0.631$ — strictly between 0 (points) and 1 (line). Paradigm of "big" sets in topology that are tiny in measure.

## 10.3 Self-Similarity

C: A set is [self-similar] if it equals a UNION OF SCALED COPIES of itself. Formally: $S = \bigcup_i f_i(S)$ where each $f_i$ is a contraction (scaling).

Q: Give examples of SELF-SIMILAR fractals with their similarity dimensions.
A: (1) [Cantor set]: 2 copies at scale 1/3 → $D = \log 2 / \log 3 \approx 0.631$. (2) [Koch snowflake] edge: 4 copies at scale 1/3 → $D = \log 4 / \log 3 \approx 1.262$. (3) [Sierpinski triangle]: 3 copies at scale 1/2 → $D = \log 3 / \log 2 \approx 1.585$. (4) [Menger sponge]: 20 copies at scale 1/3 → $D \approx 2.727$. All exactly self-similar — simplest fractals.

## 10.4 Similarity Dimension

Q: Define [similarity dimension] for self-similar sets.
A: If a set consists of $N$ copies of itself, each scaled by factor $1/s$: similarity dimension $D = \log N / \log s$. Equivalent to box-counting dimension for uniformly self-similar sets. Intuition: if you double linear resolution ($s = 2$), a 1D line needs 2× more boxes, a 2D plane needs 4×, fractional $D$ fractal needs $2^D$ boxes. "Scaling dimension" matches Hausdorff for nice fractals.

## 10.5 Iterated Function Systems (IFS)

Q: What is an [iterated function system (IFS)]?
A: A finite set of CONTRACTION MAPS $\{f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_N\}$ on a metric space. The [attractor] of the IFS is the unique non-empty compact set $S$ satisfying $S = \bigcup_i f_i(S)$. Generated by: (1) DETERMINISTIC iteration — start with any compact set, apply all $f_i$, union, repeat. (2) STOCHASTIC ("chaos game") — start with a point, randomly apply one $f_i$ per iteration; orbit accumulates on $S$. IFS create: ferns, trees, landscapes (Barnsley).

Q: Describe the [chaos game] for the Sierpinski triangle.
A: Place three vertices $V_1, V_2, V_3$ of a triangle. Start with arbitrary point $p_0$. At each step: pick a vertex $V_i$ at RANDOM (uniform); set $p_{n+1} = $ midpoint of $p_n$ and $V_i$. After many iterations, $p_n$ lies on (or very near) the SIERPINSKI TRIANGLE. Stunning: RANDOM dynamics produces DETERMINISTIC fractal. Generalizes: any IFS's attractor emerges from random selection among the maps.

## 10.6 The Mandelbrot Set

C: The [Mandelbrot set] $M$ is the set of complex numbers $c$ for which the iteration $z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c$ (starting $z_0 = 0$) does NOT escape to infinity.

Q: Describe the STRUCTURE of the Mandelbrot set.
A: A connected, complex shape in the complex plane. MAIN CARDIOID: area where iterations converge to a fixed point. Attached disks: period-2, period-3, etc. — the period-doubling bifurcations of individual $c$. Countably many "baby Mandelbrots" (smaller copies of $M$) on its boundary. BOUNDARY has fractal dimension 2 — as high as possible. Visualized via escape-time coloring: stunning images demonstrating complex dynamics' visual appeal.

## 10.7 Julia Sets

C: The [Julia set] for $f_c(z) = z^2 + c$ is the boundary of the set of points whose orbits under $f_c$ stay bounded.

Q: How are JULIA sets RELATED to the MANDELBROT set?
A: The Mandelbrot set is the set of $c$ values for which the Julia set of $f_c$ is CONNECTED. For $c \in M$: Julia set is connected (might be a deformed disk, "fat baseball"). For $c \notin M$: Julia set is a DUSTLIKE CANTOR SET (Fatou dust). The boundary of $M$ itself is the LOCUS where Julia sets change topology. Each point of $M$ parameterizes a different Julia set — infinite family of fractals.

## 10.8 Space-Filling and Pathological Fractals

Q: What is a SPACE-FILLING curve?
A: A continuous curve passing through every point of a unit square (or higher-dim region). Examples: Peano curve (1890), Hilbert curve. Topological dimension 1 but FILLS a 2D region. Shows that continuity doesn't imply low-dimensional image. Related to fractals: constructed iteratively; each iterate is an approximation filling more densely. APPLICATIONS: cache-oblivious algorithms, R-trees, dithering.

Q: Give an example of a CONTINUOUS BUT NOWHERE-DIFFERENTIABLE function.
A: The [Weierstrass function] $W(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)$ for $0 < a < 1, ab > 1 + 3\pi/2$. Continuous everywhere, differentiable nowhere. Its graph is a fractal of dimension $2 - \log a / \log b$. Historically shocking (1872) — refuting the then-prevalent belief that continuous functions are differentiable "almost everywhere." Precursor to fractal theory.

## 10.9 Physical Fractals

Q: Give EXAMPLES of naturally occurring fractals.
A: (1) [Coastlines] — dimension $\approx 1.25$ (Britain, Mandelbrot). (2) [Trees, ferns, broccoli] — branching patterns. (3) [Lung alveoli and blood vessels] — branching for surface area maximization. (4) [River networks] — Horton's laws for stream hierarchy. (5) [Clouds] — turbulent fluid dynamics. (6) [Mountain profiles] — erosion processes. (7) [Polymer coils, percolation clusters, DLA (diffusion-limited aggregation)]. Nature exhibits APPROXIMATE fractality over finite scale ranges.

## 10.10 Fractals and Dynamical Systems

Q: How do FRACTALS arise in dynamical systems?
A: (1) [Strange attractors] — chaotic attractors are fractal. (2) [Basin boundaries] — can be fractal ("fractal basin boundaries") making long-term prediction impossible. (3) [Invariant sets] of horseshoe maps — Cantor-set-like. (4) [Julia and Mandelbrot sets] — parameter space of iterated polynomials. (5) [Self-similar bifurcation cascades] — Feigenbaum universality has fractal structure in parameter space. Fractality signals chaos; both emerge from nonlinear dynamics.

## 10.11 Multifractals

Q: What is a [multifractal]?
A: A fractal with SPATIALLY VARYING local scaling: different regions have different local dimensions. Characterized by a SPECTRUM $f(\alpha)$ — the "fractal dimension of points with singularity $\alpha$". Examples: turbulent energy dissipation, financial time series, earthquakes, biological growth patterns. Richer description than a single dimension. Multifractal analysis: from data, extract $f(\alpha)$ via wavelets or box-counting generalizations.

## 10.12 A Worked Fractal Dimension

P: Compute the fractal (similarity) dimension of the KOCH SNOWFLAKE.
S:
**IDENTIFY**: The Koch curve is a self-similar fractal built by iterating: replace each line segment with 4 segments of 1/3 the length, forming a bump.

**PLAN**: Count number of copies $N$ and scaling factor $s$; apply $D = \log N / \log s$.

**EXECUTE**:
One iteration of the Koch construction: take a line segment of length 1. Replace with:
- One segment of length 1/3 (left third),
- One segment of length 1/3 (rising at 60°),
- One segment of length 1/3 (falling at 60°),
- One segment of length 1/3 (right third).

So the new curve consists of $N = 4$ segments, each scaled by $1/s = 1/3$ (original length 1 becomes four pieces each 1/3 long).

At the next iteration, each of these 4 segments undergoes the same transformation — 4 copies of the Koch curve at scale 1/3 recover the full curve. Hence:

$D = \frac{\log 4}{\log 3} = \frac{2 \log 2}{\log 3} \approx \frac{1.386}{1.099} \approx 1.262$.

Length: each iteration multiplies total length by $4/3$. After $n$ iterations: length $= (4/3)^n \to \infty$ — INFINITE LENGTH bounding FINITE AREA (snowflake).

**EVALUATE**: Dimension $D \approx 1.262$ lies strictly between 1 (line) and 2 (plane) — "more than a line, less than a surface." The snowflake's boundary is a fractal of infinite length surrounding a region of finite area ($2\sqrt{3}/5 \cdot a^2$ for initial triangle side $a$). Remarkable: area finite, perimeter infinite — impossible for smooth curves. Gives intuition for coastlines: measured with smaller rulers, length increases without bound, reflecting fractal boundary structure. Fractal dimension quantifies exactly this "roughness."
